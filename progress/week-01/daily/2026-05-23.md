## 📚 Learnings

### Video — *"The Cross Product"* (Freya Holmér)

#### Formula

```
a × b = (ay·bz - az·by,
         az·bx - ax·bz,
         ax·by - ay·bx)
```

The result is **not a scalar** — it is a **new vector**, perpendicular to both inputs.

```
a × b = a new vector ⊥ to both a and b
```

#### Output Reference

| Input Relationship | Cross Product Result |
| --- | --- |
| Parallel vectors (0°) | Zero vector (magnitude = 0) |
| Perpendicular vectors (90°) | Maximum magnitude |
| Order reversed: b × a | Same magnitude, opposite direction |

#### Practical Analogy

Imagine holding two sticks flat on a table, pointing in different directions. The cross product is the nail you drive straight *down through the table* — perpendicular to both sticks at once. Change which stick is "first" and the nail flips to point straight *up*. Same nail, same position, completely opposite direction. That sign change is not a quirk — it is the entire point.

#### Primary Applications in Games / 3D

- **Surface normals** — determining which direction a polygon faces (critical for lighting)
- **Torque / rotation** — computing the direction of angular force in physics simulations
- **Camera orientation** — deriving the "up" and "right" vectors as camera orientation changes
- **Side detection** — determining whether a point lies to the left or right of a line, without angle math

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

- The cross product produces a **vector**, not a scalar — this is the fundamental difference from dot product
- With the Z-component sign alone, you can replace `atan2()` / `acos()` for left-right detection — a significant performance gain at scale
- Cross product only works in 3D. The wedge product is the generalized version that works across all dimensions
- The dot product asks *"how parallel?"* — the cross product asks *"how perpendicular?"* — they are two sides of the same geometric product in Geometric Algebra
- Every camera system in every game engine — UE5, Unity, all of them — runs on cross product silently, every frame

## 💡 Deeper Notes — Insights & Interesting Connections

### 1. Cross Product Is the Complement of Dot Product — Not a Standalone Operation

After studying dot product yesterday (scalar result = *how aligned* two vectors are), cross product completes the picture: **how perpendicular two vectors are**. In Geometric Algebra, they are not two separate operations — they are the symmetric and anti-symmetric parts of a single geometric product. Dot product takes the symmetric half. Cross product takes the anti-symmetric half.

Shader implication: if dot product measures *how much light hits a surface*, cross product determines *which direction that surface faces relative to a light source* — two complementary quantities that together form the foundation of any real-time lighting model.

> Knowing both operations means you stop thinking about lighting as "just shaders" and start seeing it as geometry — and geometry you can reason about, modify, and debug.

---

### 2. The Z-Component Is a Compass — Without Trigonometry

To know whether something is to the left or right of a reference direction, no angle calculation is needed at all. Just read the sign of the Z component from the cross product result. Positive = right. Negative = left.

This is computationally far cheaper than `atan2()` or `acos()`, both of which involve expensive trigonometric lookups. In a game running hundreds of enemies — each checking relative positions every frame — that difference is non-trivial.

> The cross product gives you directional information without ever asking *what is the angle*. That is a shift in how you think about spatial relationships.

---

### 3. Cross Product Is the Foundation of Every Camera System

Every time a camera moves in a game, the engine silently computes three vectors: **forward**, **right**, **up**. The right vector and up vector are derived via cross product between the forward vector and the world up `(0, 1, 0)`. Without cross product, a camera cannot know its own "right" or "up" as orientation changes.

This is not Unity-specific or UE5-specific. It is true of every engine that uses a view matrix — which is all of them.

> I have been moving cameras in games my entire life without knowing this was happening. Now I cannot un-see it.

---

### 4. Relationship to Bivectors — Cross Product as a Projected Wedge Product

In *"Why can't you multiply vectors?"*, Freya explains that the wedge product `(a ∧ b)` produces a **bivector** — an oriented area between two vectors. The cross product is a 3D-specific method of extracting a single vector from that bivector, using duality.

The critical distinction: **cross product only works in 3D**. The wedge product generalizes to any number of dimensions.

This matters for later work. When debugging complex shaders or physics simulations, knowing that cross product = *the 3D version of the wedge product* explains *why* certain operations break or behave unexpectedly at dimension boundaries.

> Freya keeps showing the same idea from different angles. I am starting to think that is the whole point — the math is one coherent thing, and every operation is just a view of it.

---

### 5. Rotation Is a Cross Product Running Continuously

When an object rotates, every point on its surface moves in a direction perpendicular to both the rotation axis and the point's position — exactly what cross product defines.

```
velocity = ω × position
```

Every rotation animation in an engine, every quaternion rotor, every angular velocity in a physics simulation — all of it is cross product running underneath.

> Yesterday I learned the dot product is inside every lighting calculation. Today I learned the cross product is inside every rotation. The math I used to skip in school is running inside everything I want to build.
